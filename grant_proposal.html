Section 1

Executive Summary

Description of the Discovery

Drug-resistant epilepsy (DRE) affects roughly one in three epilepsy patients — over 1.1 million people in North America alone.[1] These patients require complex evaluations in specialized Epilepsy Monitoring Units (EMUs), where doctors record days of continuous brain-wave video (video-EEG), brain scans (MRI, PET, SPECT), and, for some, data from surgically implanted brain stimulators. The problem is that each of these data types is trapped in a separate, proprietary computer system that cannot communicate with the others. Doctors must manually piece together information across five or more disconnected systems for each patient, a process that contributes to a median delay of 16.7 years from diagnosis to potentially life-saving surgery.[2]

Our invention is a vendor-neutral, cloud-based data platform that breaks down these silos. Using a "Fit-for-Purpose" architecture that combines three complementary health data standards — DICOM for medical images and EEG waveforms, openEHR for deep clinical data storage, and HL7 FHIR for interoperable data exchange — the platform ingests data from all major EEG vendors (Natus, Nihon Kohden, Persyst), imaging systems, and neuromodulation devices (NeuroPace RNS, Medtronic DBS, LivaNova VNS) into a single, unified patient timeline. It compresses multi-day video-EEG recordings using H.265/HEVC encoding (reducing storage by 50% over current methods) and presents the complete clinical picture through a web-based viewer accessible to the entire surgical planning team.[3]

Description of the Business Opportunity

No commercial product currently unifies video-EEG, brain imaging, and neuromodulation data across vendor boundaries.[4] The target market includes approximately 250 Level 4 epilepsy centers in the US and 20 comprehensive programs in Canada, each spending $35,000–$40,000 per EMU admission with 50,000–60,000 annual admissions nationally.[5] The global EEG market is valued at $1.3–$1.7 billion (2024) with 5–10% annual growth.[6] Operating as a Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) platform priced at $40,000–$100,000 per center annually, the core North American addressable market for EMU data management is $15–$65 million, with expansion into ambulatory EEG, neuromodulation clinics, and pharmaceutical research partnerships. The platform initially launches as a Medical Device Data System (MDDS) — a non-device classification under both US and Canadian regulation — avoiding costly FDA 510(k) clearance while building the data infrastructure necessary for future AI-powered diagnostic modules.[7]

Justification for Grant Funding

Grant funding is critical to de-risk the 2-month prototype phase by funding development of the core Extraction-Transformation-Linking (ETL) pipelines that normalize proprietary vendor data into open standards. The commercial risk is low: there are no competitors in this specific niche, the clinical need is documented across multiple peer-reviewed publications, and the regulatory pathway is clear. A non-dilutive funding stack of $100,000–$245,000 is achievable in Year 1 through NRC IRAP ($30K–$50K via Accelerated Review), SR&ED tax credits ($35K–$65K retroactive), and academic partnerships through Mitacs and NSERC Alliance grants.[8] The prototype directly addresses the most common reason cited by Canadian neurologists for epilepsy surgery underutilization: inadequate healthcare resources, reported by 75% of surveyed neurologists.[9] Every year of delayed surgery exposes patients to a 3.7–9.0 per 1,000 risk of sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP) — a risk that surgery reduces three-fold.[10]

Section 2

Research and Technical Plan

2.1 Background of the Discovery

The diagnostic workup for drug-resistant epilepsy is among the most data-intensive processes in clinical medicine. A single 5-day EMU admission generates 500 GB to 2+ TB of combined data: 72–120 hours of continuous video-EEG (multi-channel waveforms at 256–2,048 Hz sampling with synchronized dual-camera 1080p video), structural and functional neuroimaging (MRI, fMRI, PET, SPECT, MEG), neuropsychological assessments, and clinical histories spanning years to decades.[11] Reviewing this data requires 3–5 hours of epileptologist time per admission — a staggering burden given that Canada has only approximately 854 neurologists total, with a "strong risk of shortage" projected through 2033.[12]

The fundamental problem is vendor-driven data fragmentation. EEG data is locked in proprietary formats: Nihon Kohden's .EEG, Natus's binary format, Compumedics ProFusion, and Cadwell's Easy III, each requiring vendor-specific review software.[13] Neuromodulation device data is entirely siloed — NeuroPace's Patient Data Management System (PDMS) stores electrocorticographic recordings in a proprietary cloud accessible only through NeuroPace portals; Medtronic's Percept PC stores local field potentials on-device with no remote access; LivaNova's VNS data is accessible only during in-office programming visits.[14] Brain imaging lives in PACS systems that cannot display EEG waveforms, and EEG systems cannot display imaging. No existing platform bridges these silos.

This fragmentation directly contributes to the catastrophic surgical referral delay. In Canada, the median delay from epilepsy diagnosis to surgery is 16.7 years, with rural patients in Saskatchewan waiting a median of 18.2 years.[2] Only 1.2% of Ontario's medically intractable epilepsy patients underwent surgery within 2 years between 2001 and 2010.[15] The province had only 19 permanent EMU beds (6 pediatric, 13 adult) as of a 2012 expert panel review, which recommended expansion to 50.[16] Seven of Canada's thirteen provinces and territories have zero EMU capacity.[17]

Internationally, no existing software product solves this problem. Persyst, the most widely deployed EEG analysis software (used at 97 of the top 100 US neurology hospitals), supports approximately 70 input formats but outputs only to its own viewer — it does not integrate neuromodulation data or manage the complete presurgical workflow.[4] The only academic prototype approaching unification is INTUITION (University of Illinois Chicago, 2023), which integrates EHR, neuroimaging, and EEG for epilepsy surgery patients at a single center, but it is not a deployable commercial platform.[18]

2.2 Summary of the Relevant Literature

The literature supporting this platform spans five domains: clinical workflow burden, informatics standards, AI-assisted diagnostics, chronotherapy, and health record architecture.

Clinical workflow and data burden. Sahoo et al. (2015) described the "Three V challenges" of epilepsy data — volume, velocity, and variety — noting that existing neuroinformatics tools "have limited capability and do not scale."[19] Quraishi and Hirsch (2025) documented how long-term ambulatory intracranial EEG from neurostimulators (RNS, DBS) is "beginning to transform epilepsy care" but generates massive data volumes, identifying subclinical seizures vastly outnumbering patient-reported events and revealing circadian and multiday seizure cycles that inform treatment.[20] Thomas and Jobst (2015) detailed the ongoing clinical burden of NeuroPace RNS device interrogation, ECoG pattern review, and stimulation parameter adjustment, with the device achieving approximately 50% seizure reduction at 2 years.[21] The ASET (American Society of EEG Technologists) position statement confirmed that technologists must edit and consolidate EEG recordings every 2–3 hours to manage data volume, and vendor-specific review systems create persistent workflow bottlenecks.[22]

Epilepsy surgery efficacy and underutilization. Wiebe et al.'s landmark randomized controlled trial (NEJM, 2001) demonstrated 58% seizure freedom with surgery versus 8% with medical management (p<0.001).[23] The ERSET trial (Engel et al., JAMA, 2012) showed 73% seizure freedom with early surgery versus 0% with continued medical therapy in mesial temporal lobe epilepsy.[24] Yet Engel (2018) estimated that fewer than 1% of eligible DRE patients undergo timely surgery.[25] Rheims et al. (Epilepsia, 2022) quantified that epilepsy surgery reduces SUDEP risk three-fold: 2.1 per 1,000 patient-years in operated versus 6.6 in non-operated patients (OR 0.32, 95% CI 0.19–0.56).[10]

Informatics standards for epilepsy. DICOM Supplement 217 (2020) established the first neurophysiology waveform standard within DICOM, defining Information Object Definitions for routine scalp EEG with standardized electrode naming and annotation contexts.[26] Supplement 239 (2024) added Waveform Annotation Structured Reports enabling multi-reviewer seizure annotation,[27] and Supplement 236 (2025) encoded montage and filter settings as Waveform Presentation State objects.[28] Critical gaps remain: no IODs exist for long-term monitoring EEG, intracranial EEG, video-EEG synchronization, or high-density EEG — representing a standards-based market opportunity.[26] The Epilepsy and Seizure Ontology (EpSO), developed at Case Western Reserve University, provides OWL2-based formal encoding of seizure semiology, epilepsy syndromes, and anatomical localization, with demonstrated improvements in diagnostic accuracy.[29]

Problem-oriented medical records and health record architecture. Lawrence Weed's Problem-Oriented Medical Record (POMR) framework — problem list, SOAP notes, structured database, and linked plans — maps directly to epilepsy care, where the problem list captures each seizure type and comorbidity, the Objective component records EEG and imaging findings, and the Assessment synthesizes concordance for surgical planning.[30] Cimino's twelve desiderata for controlled medical vocabularies (1998) provide the evaluation framework for terminology selection: SNOMED CT satisfies the most desiderata (concept orientation, polyhierarchy, formal definitions, nonsemantic identifiers) but has critical gaps in epilepsy-specific concepts like high-frequency oscillations and seizure onset patterns.[31] ISO 18308 defines requirements for EHR architectures including longitudinal records, multimedia data types, audit trails, and consent management — all directly applicable to the platform.[32]

Chronotherapy and adaptive neurostimulation. Circadian rhythms profoundly influence epileptic networks. Baud et al. (2018, Nature Communications) demonstrated multidien (multi-day) seizure cycles using chronic intracranial EEG from RNS devices.[33] Adjusting neurostimulation parameters based on time-of-day and anti-seizure medication pharmacokinetics represents a future application of the longitudinal data the platform would aggregate — a secondary innovation path requiring the data infrastructure proposed here as a prerequisite.[34]

2.3 Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

True vendor neutrality. By ingesting data from all major EEG systems (Natus, Nihon Kohden, Persyst, Compumedics, Cadwell), imaging systems (any DICOM-compliant PACS), and neuromodulation devices, the platform eliminates the vendor lock-in that currently fragments epilepsy care. Hospitals can select best-of-breed equipment without data compatibility concerns.[4]

Standards-based persistence with the "Fit-for-Purpose" architecture. Using openEHR's two-level modeling paradigm for long-term clinical data storage and FHIR for interoperable exchange creates a future-proof architecture where clinical knowledge evolves independently of software — new EEG archetype definitions or seizure classification updates deploy without code changes.[35]

Dramatic compression of review burden. Unifying the patient timeline eliminates the manual data aggregation that currently consumes 30–60 minutes per multidisciplinary conference case. H.265/HEVC video compression reduces storage requirements by 25–50% over H.264 while maintaining diagnostic quality.[36]

Early-mover advantage in DICOM neurophysiology. The 2020–2025 release of DICOM Supplements 217, 239, and 236 creates a narrow implementation window. No commercial platform has yet implemented these standards for clinical EEG-to-PACS integration. The first clinical implementation (WIGEV Epilepsy Center, Vienna, 2023) validated feasibility but remains a single-center research effort.[37]

Disadvantages

Legacy system integration complexity. Proprietary EEG formats are undocumented and vary by vendor version, requiring reverse-engineering or vendor partnership for accurate conversion. EDF+, the most common open format, is limited to 16-bit resolution and lacks metadata for intracranial electrode coordinates.[38]

Data volume and cost. Multi-day video-EEG generates 30+ GB per day; cloud storage and bandwidth for even a single Level 4 center with 3–4 EMU beds would require 15–60 TB annual storage. While tiered storage (hot/warm/cold) mitigates cost, this remains a significant infrastructure expense.[11]

Hospital procurement friction. Sales cycles to hospital IT departments average 9–24 months with approximately 9 stakeholders per purchasing decision (CIO, CMIO, department chair, IT security, compliance, finance, procurement, clinical end-users).[39]

Regulatory complexity for future AI features. Any signal processing or automated interpretation (seizure detection, spike detection, artifact removal) immediately exits the MDDS exclusion and enters FDA 510(k) / Health Canada Class II–III regulation, with dense patent thickets from Ceribell and Persyst.[7]

2.4 Research Questions

The following scientific and technical questions must be resolved to advance the platform toward commercialization:

RQ1: Can proprietary, legacy EEG data formats (Nihon Kohden .EEG, Natus binary, Compumedics ProFusion) be accurately and losslessly converted into standardized DICOM Supplement 217 neurophysiology waveform objects, preserving all channel metadata, annotations, and temporal synchronization?

RQ2: What H.265/HEVC compression parameters (quantization parameter, GOP structure, profile/level) achieve optimal compression of multi-day synchronized video-EEG for streaming over standard hospital networks (1 Gbps) without degrading the facial semiology and behavioral observations essential for seizure classification?

RQ3: Can the "Fit-for-Purpose" multi-standard architecture (openEHR persistence + FHIR exchange + DICOM waveforms/imaging) reduce manual data aggregation time by ≥80% during multidisciplinary epilepsy surgical conferences compared to current vendor-siloed workflows?

RQ4: What openEHR archetype definitions are required to capture the complete presurgical epilepsy evaluation dataset — including seizure semiology, interictal/ictal EEG findings, electrode coordinates, stimulation mapping results, neuropsychological scores, and ILAE 2017 classification data — and how do these archetypes map to FHIR resources for exchange?

2.5 Research and Methodologies

Hypothesis

Implementing a Fit-for-Purpose multi-standard EMR stack using openEHR archetypes for clinical data persistence, FHIR R4 resources for interoperable exchange, and DICOM Supplement 217/239/236 for waveform and imaging data will reduce the time clinicians spend aggregating multimodal neurodiagnostic data for presurgical epilepsy conferences by at least 80%, while achieving lossless fidelity in format conversion as validated by round-trip comparison of waveform amplitude, timing, and annotation data.

Experimental Plan

Phase 1 — ETL Pipeline Development (Weeks 1–4). We will develop agentic AI-assisted ETL (Extraction-Transformation-Linking) pipelines to ingest the three most prevalent proprietary EEG formats in Canadian epilepsy centers: Nihon Kohden (.EEG/.21E), Natus/Xltek (.erd/.eeg), and EDF+. Each pipeline will: (a) parse the proprietary binary format to extract channel data, sampling rates, electrode labels, annotations, and event markers; (b) normalize to an intermediate representation conforming to the openEHR EEG observation archetype; (c) emit DICOM Supplement 217 Routine Scalp EEG IODs for PACS integration and FHIR Observation resources for EHR interoperability. Validation will compare source and converted waveforms using mean absolute error (MAE < 0.1 µV), cross-correlation (r > 0.999), and annotation timestamp alignment (±1 sample). Sample datasets will be obtained from publicly available EEG repositories (TUH EEG Corpus, CHB-MIT) and, with ethics approval, from a partnering Canadian epilepsy center.

Phase 2 — Video Compression and Streaming (Weeks 3–6). We will evaluate H.265/HEVC encoding against H.264/AVC baseline for multi-day video-EEG using the FFmpeg x265 encoder. Test parameters: QP values 18–35 across Main and Main10 profiles, GOP sizes of 30–120 frames, at 1080p 30fps input. Metrics: (a) compression ratio versus storage baseline; (b) structural similarity index (SSIM ≥ 0.95 required); (c) clinical validation by an epileptologist reviewing 10 seizure events at each compression level to confirm preservation of facial semiology (head version, ictal grimacing, perioral automatisms, eye deviation). Adaptive bitrate streaming (HLS) will be implemented for browser-based review with target latency <3 seconds on 100 Mbps hospital networks.

Phase 3 — FHIR/openEHR Integration Layer (Weeks 5–8). We will implement a SMART on FHIR application using EHR Launch workflow (OAuth 2.0 authorization, scopes: patient/Observation.rs, patient/DiagnosticReport.rs, patient/ImagingStudy.rs) for integration with Epic and Cerner/Oracle Health test environments. The openEHR persistence layer will use an open-source CDR (EHRBase or Better Platform) with custom-authored archetypes for: EEG recording observation, seizure event evaluation, electrode placement cluster, and neurostimulator device data. The FHIR Consent resource will manage granular patient permissions with recursive provision structures for research data sharing. A POMR-based clinical interface will present the unified patient timeline organized by problem list, with SOAP-structured progress notes linked to specific EEG epochs and imaging findings.

Expertise. The founding team combines health informatics expertise in FHIR, DICOM, and openEHR with clinical domain knowledge from academic epilepsy center collaboration. The Mitacs internship component will engage a graduate student in biomedical informatics or computer science for the archetype development and FHIR profiling work.

2.6 Health Data Standards and Communication Protocols

The architecture employs a strict "Fit-for-Purpose" multi-standard stack, where each standard is applied to the task for which it was designed, consistent with the emerging consensus in health informatics that no single standard adequately covers all requirements.[35]

HL7 Standards: From RIM to FHIR

The HL7 Reference Information Model (RIM), the normative foundation of HL7 Version 3, defines six backbone classes — Act, Entity, Role, Participation, ActRelationship, and RoleLink — that model all healthcare information.[40] An EMU encounter maps to Act[classCode=ENC], the patient to Entity[PSN]+Role[PAT], and clinical team participations through typed Participation links (ATND for epileptologist, PRF for EEG technologist). While the RIM provides rigorous semantic grounding, its complexity limited HL7 v3 adoption. FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources, R4 normative since 2019) replaced RIM's abstract framework with ~150 concrete Resource types accessible via RESTful APIs in JSON/XML.[41] The platform uses FHIR R4 as the external API layer, with key resource mappings: Patient (RIM: Entity+Role), Encounter (Act[ENC]), Observation (Act[OBS]) for EEG findings, DiagnosticReport for structured EEG reports, Condition for the epilepsy diagnosis (problem list), ImagingStudy for DICOM references, and Consent for privacy management. SMART on FHIR v2.2.0 provides the authorization framework with OAuth 2.0 scopes, mandated by ONC for certified US EHR technology.[42]

openEHR: Persistence Layer with Two-Level Modeling

openEHR's two-level modeling paradigm separates the stable technical Reference Model (Level 1) from clinical knowledge defined in archetypes (Level 2).[43] Five ENTRY subtypes — OBSERVATION, EVALUATION, INSTRUCTION, ACTION, ADMIN_ENTRY — derive from the Clinical Investigator Recording ontology, synthesized from Weed's problem-oriented method. Archetypes are defined in the Archetype Definition Language (ADL 2.0) using constraint expressions (cADL) that specify data types, cardinalities, value ranges, and terminology bindings to SNOMED CT or LOINC. Templates compose multiple archetypes for specific use cases — the EMU encounter template nests OBSERVATION.imaging_exam_result for MRI findings, a custom EEG observation archetype, EVALUATION.problem_diagnosis for the epilepsy diagnosis, ACTION.procedure for the monitoring procedure, and CLUSTER.device for neuromodulation devices.[44] openEHR serves as the internal clinical data repository with AQL (Archetype Query Language) querying, while FHIR provides the external interoperability API — a complementary architecture increasingly endorsed by both communities.[35]

DICOM Neurophysiology Standards

DICOM Supplement 217 (2020) defines IODs for routine scalp EEG with 8-bit or 16-bit samples, channel-multiplexed encoding, unconstrained sampling frequency, standardized electrode naming (CID 3030), and annotation context groups (CID 3040).[26] Supplement 239 (2024) enables separate annotation objects referencing waveform data, supporting modality-specific annotations (spikes, bursts, seizure events) with temporal referencing at timepoints or epochs and channel-specific targeting — essential for multi-reviewer seizure annotation workflows.[27] Supplement 236 (2025) encodes visualization montage and filter settings as separate DICOM objects, supporting Laplacian derivations and time-varying montage per epoch — enabling reproducible EEG review across institutions for presurgical evaluation.[28] The platform implements these supplements while bridging critical gaps: no long-term monitoring EEG IOD (draft in progress), no intracranial EEG IOD, and no video-EEG synchronization IOD.[26]

Terminology Standards and Cimino's Desiderata

Evaluated against Cimino's twelve desiderata for controlled medical vocabularies,[31] SNOMED CT provides the strongest backbone (polyhierarchy, formal description-logic definitions, nonsemantic identifiers, concept permanence), with approximately 370,000+ active concepts. However, critical epilepsy-specific gaps persist — no representation of high-frequency oscillations (HFOs), seizure onset patterns from stereo-EEG, or the ILAE 2017 reclassified seizure types. The Epilepsy and Seizure Ontology (EpSO) fills these gaps with OWL2-based formal encoding.[29] LOINC provides observation identifiers (e.g., LOINC 11524-6 for EEG study). ICD-10-CM encodes the diagnosis: G40.x19 codes capture intractable (drug-resistant) epilepsy by seizure type, where the 5th character "1" denotes intractability — though ICD-10 still uses abandoned terminology ("complex partial" rather than ILAE's "focal impaired awareness").[45] ICD-11 restructures epilepsy under block 8A6 with post-coordination capability, better aligning with the ILAE 2017 classification.[46] UMLS provides cross-vocabulary mapping through Concept Unique Identifiers (CUIs) — CUI C1096063 maps "Drug resistant epilepsy" across SNOMED CT (79348005), ICD-10-CM (G40.x1x), and MeSH (D000069279).[47] MeSH serves as the literature indexing layer (descriptor: Drug Resistant Epilepsy, D000069279, established 2015). UCUM provides units of measurement for medication dosing and physiological measurements.

IHE Integration Profiles

IHE Cross-Enterprise Document Sharing (XDS) provides the document-centric architecture for sharing EEG reports and imaging across institutions. Cross-Community Access (XCA) extends this across Affinity Domains via Initiating/Responding Gateways — directly applicable to multi-site Canadian epilepsy data sharing. Privacy Consent on FHIR (PCF v1.1.0) profiles the FHIR Consent resource with RBAC/ABAC enforcement and automated security labeling — critical for PIPEDA/PHIPA compliance.[48]

Communication Protocols

The platform supports: HL7 v2.x ADT (admit/discharge/transfer) and ORU (observation results) messages for legacy EHR integration; FHIR RESTful API (CRUD + search) over HTTPS/TLS 1.3; DICOM DIMSE C-STORE for PACS push/pull; DICOMweb (WADO-RS, STOW-RS) for web-based imaging access; and OAuth 2.0/OpenID Connect for authentication. All data in transit is encrypted with TLS 1.3; data at rest uses AES-256 encryption with customer-managed keys.

Implementation Plan for Demoable Prototype

3.1 Regulatory Requirements

United States — MDDS Exclusion. Under the 21st Century Cures Act §520(o)(1)(D), software for "transferring, storing, converting formats, or displaying" clinical or device data qualifies for the Medical Device Data System (MDDS) exclusion, provided it does not interpret or analyze data to drive clinical decisions.[7] The prototype's intended use — ingesting proprietary EEG data, converting to DICOM/FHIR standards, compressing video, and displaying the unified patient timeline for independent clinician review — falls squarely within MDDS scope. The January 2026 FDA CDS Final Guidance confirms that software processing medical signals (EEG) or medical images (MRI) fails Criterion 1 of the Non-Device CDS exemption, meaning any future AI features (seizure detection, spike detection, artifact removal) will require 510(k) clearance under product code OMB (21 CFR 882.1400, Class II).[49]

Canada — Health Canada SaMD Classification. Health Canada's IMDRF-aligned framework classifies SaMD by significance of the information provided and the severity of the healthcare situation. For epilepsy (serious-to-critical condition), a data management/visualization platform that "informs clinical management" maps to Class II under Schedule 1, Rule 10(1), requiring a Medical Device Licence (MDL), MDSAP-certified quality system, and 15-day screening plus 60-day review.[50] A potential exclusion pathway exists under the Medical Devices Regulations for software that merely displays medical information to support independent HCP review — however, any signal processing (filtering, montage computation, artifact reduction) fails this exclusion. A Medical Device Establishment Licence (MDEL) is required for Canadian manufacturers ($5,426 fee, 25% small-business reduction).[51]

Quality System Requirements. ISO 13485:2016 certification is mandatory for the Canadian market, with MDSAP (Medical Device Single Audit Program) mandatory for Class II+ MDL since January 2019. Year 1 costs: $25K–$80K for consulting, audit, training, and QMS software. SOC 2 Type II certification ($30K–$80K initial) is a practical requirement for US healthcare customer procurement.[52]

3.2 Security and Privacy Protection

The platform must comply with the US Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), Canada's Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA), and provincial legislation including Ontario's Personal Health Information Protection Act (PHIPA) and Quebec's Law 25.[53]

HIPAA compliance requires Business Associate Agreements with every covered entity client, encryption (AES-256 at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit), access controls, and immutable audit logs. Video-EEG always constitutes identifiable PHI under Safe Harbor identifier #17 (full-face photographs). Brain MRI poses re-identification risk — studies demonstrate 83–97% facial recognition accuracy from volumetric MRI.[54] For secondary research use, the platform supports both Safe Harbor de-identification (removal of 18 identifier categories) and Expert Determination (§164.514(b)(1)). AI-based facial de-identification (cartoonization or face-swapping per Hou et al. 2023, Dufau et al. 2024) preserves seizure semiology while reducing re-identification risk.[55]

PHIPA compliance relies on implied consent within the "circle of care" for treatment purposes (PHIPA ss. 20(2)–20(3)) but requires express consent for disclosure to the SaaS platform as a non-HIC third party. The platform implements a dynamic consent interface using the FHIR Consent resource (R4 recursive provision structure) allowing patients to granularly control access: permit/deny by data type (EEG waveforms, video, imaging, clinical notes), by purpose (TREAT, HRESCH, HPAYMT), by actor, and by time period.[56] PHIPA Section 44 permits PHI disclosure for research without individual consent under strict conditions: written research plan, REB approval, and data sharing agreement.[57]

Data residency: All Canadian patient data hosted in AWS Canada (Montreal) region with customer-managed KMS keys. RBAC enforces five roles (epileptologist, neurosurgeon, EEG technologist, nurse, researcher) with ABAC policies governing resource-type access, de-identification requirements, network location, and purpose-of-use. All access events logged to FHIR AuditEvent resources conforming to ISO 18308 and IHE ATNA profile.[32]

3.3 Intellectual Property and Freedom to Operate

IP Strategy. The platform's proprietary ETL pipelines (vendor-specific format parsing, normalization algorithms, and agentic AI orchestration) and openEHR archetype definitions for epilepsy neurodiagnostics will be protected as trade secrets. Novel methodologies for multi-modal data coregistration (3D electrode localization overlaid on brain MRI) will be evaluated for software patent protection. The Fit-for-Purpose architecture itself — the specific integration of openEHR persistence + FHIR exchange + DICOM neurophysiology waveforms — represents a novel system design.

Freedom to Operate (FTO) Assessment. Cloud-based data storage, format conversion, and visualization carry low infringement risk — no blocking patents were identified for MDDS-classified data management platforms. Multi-vendor data ingestion carries moderate risk due to proprietary format structures (not patent-protected but contractually restricted). AI-based seizure detection carries high risk due to Ceribell's broad US10,743,809 covering ML-based seizure detection pipelines. AI-based artifact removal carries moderate-high risk due to Persyst's BSS-CCA patents (US11,317,871).[58] Several foundational patents have recently expired, expanding the public domain design space: NeuroPace US6,810,285 (~2024), US6,230,049 (~2020), Persyst US6,735,467 (~2022).[58]

Strategic positioning: By launching as MDDS (data transfer/storage/display without interpretation), the platform avoids the densest patent thickets. Future AI features will employ transformer-based architectures and attention mechanisms distinct from the patented feature-extraction pipelines (ensemble gradient-boosted trees, CNNs, LSTMs).[58]

3.5 Market

Target market: ~250 NAEC-accredited Level 4 epilepsy centers in the US and ~20 comprehensive epilepsy programs in Canada, encompassing approximately 26–29 active EMUs with an average of 3.6 beds each in Canada alone.[17] Secondary targets: ~350 Level 3 centers, ambulatory EEG providers, and neuromodulation clinics.

Market size: Core North American TAM for EMU data management: $15M–$65M annually at $40K–$100K per center. The global EEG market ($1.3–$1.7B, CAGR 5–10%) and neurotech investment ($2.3B in 2024 across 129 deals) provide strong tailwinds.[6]

Barriers: (1) Entrenched legacy vendors (Natus, Nihon Kohden) requiring proof of workflow efficiency gains to force switching; (2) 9–24 month hospital sales cycles; (3) data migration complexity for legacy archives; (4) provincial health privacy variation in Canada (Ontario PHIPA vs. Quebec Law 25 vs. Alberta HIA). Regulatory barrier: ISO 13485/MDSAP certification required before Canadian MDL application (9–18 month timeline, $25K–$80K).[52]

Competitive advantage: No existing product unifies video-EEG + brain imaging + neuromodulation data across vendor boundaries. Persyst supports ~70 EEG formats but does not integrate imaging or device data. NeuroPace PDMS is RNS-only. PACS vendors archive but cannot analyze EEG. The platform's interoperability layer — bridging DICOM (clinical), BIDS (research), and FHIR (exchange) — occupies a unique standards convergence point.[4]

3.7 Impact

Economic impact. The platform addresses a $15M–$65M North American TAM with high-margin recurring SaaS revenue and no direct competitor. By reducing manual data aggregation (currently 30–60 minutes per conference case) and enabling faster presurgical evaluation, each center increases patient throughput and reduces per-case costs. Alberta data shows healthcare costs escalate by CAD $341 every 6 months before EMU admission but fall by CAD $802 every 6 months after — and surgery patients see costs drop by half.[59] The estimated Canadian national economic burden of epilepsy is CAD $3.4–$4.5 billion annually.[60]

Clinical and social impact. Every year of surgical delay exposes DRE patients to 3.7–9.0 per 1,000 risk of SUDEP — surgery reduces this three-fold.[10] With only 1.2% of eligible Ontario patients reaching surgery within 2 years,[15] even a modest acceleration of the presurgical evaluation pipeline would save lives. Helmstaedter et al. (2003) showed that 50–60% of temporal lobe epilepsy patients experience progressive memory decline, but seizure freedom can halt and even reverse cognitive deterioration.[61] The platform enables the multidisciplinary team to synthesize concordant data faster, directly reducing time-to-surgical-decision. For the over 1.1 million North American DRE patients, this infrastructure is not merely a workflow improvement — it is the prerequisite for closing the gap between the availability and utilization of life-saving epilepsy surgery.
